Why 6 Feet? The Origins Explained

The tradition of burying the deceased 6 feet under dates back to the 17th century, particularly during the plague years in London. In 1665, the Lord Mayor of London issued an order that graves should be dug at least 6 feet deep to help prevent the spread of disease. This depth was believed to be sufficient to keep animals from disturbing the graves and to minimize the risk of contamination from decomposing bodies. Over time, this practice became embedded in Western burial customs, even as the original health concerns faded.

Modern Burial Practices

Today, the actual depth of graves can vary depending on local laws, cemetery regulations, and soil conditions. While 6 feet remains a common standard in many areas, some graves are dug slightly shallower or deeper. Modern burial vaults and liners also play a role in maintaining grave integrity and protecting remains. Cultural and Legal Considerations

Not all cultures or religions require burial at exactly 6 feet. Some traditions call for shallower graves, while others may use mausoleums or above-ground crypts. Local laws often dictate minimum depths to ensure safety and sanitation. In many places, the use of burial vaults or grave liners is required, which can affect how deep the grave must be dug. Always check with your local cemetery or funeral director to understand the specific requirements in your area.
