What Happens During Cremation?

Cremation is a carefully regulated process that transforms the body into ashes using intense heat. The deceased is placed in a cremation chamber, often called a retort, where temperatures reach between 1400 and 1800 degrees Fahrenheit. The process typically takes two to three hours, depending on the size of the body and the type of container used. After the cremation is complete, any remaining bone fragments are processed into a fine, sand-like consistency, which is then placed in a temporary or permanent urn for the family.

Preparation and Identification

Before cremation begins, funeral professionals follow strict identification protocols to ensure that each individual is properly tracked throughout the process. Personal items such as jewelry or medical devices are removed, and the body is placed in a combustible container. Families may request a final viewing or private farewell before the cremation takes place, depending on the facility and local regulations.

Aftercare and Memorial Options

Once the ashes are returned to the family, there are many ways to honor a loved one. Some choose to keep the urn at home, while others opt for burial in a cemetery or scattering in a meaningful location. Memorial services can be held before or after cremation, allowing families to celebrate a life in a way that feels right for them. Cremation offers flexibility and personalization, making it a fitting choice for many modern families.
