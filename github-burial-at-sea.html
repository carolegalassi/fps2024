Burial At Sea: A Complete Guide

Burial at sea is a time-honored tradition that offers a unique and meaningful way to say farewell to loved ones. Whether chosen for personal, religious, or environmental reasons, this ceremony provides a peaceful setting surrounded by the vastness of the ocean. In this guide, we will explore what is involved in a burial at sea, the legal requirements, and how families can plan a respectful and memorable service.

What Is Burial At Sea?

Burial at sea refers to the act of committing a body or cremated remains to the ocean. This practice has deep roots in maritime history and is recognized by many cultures and religions. Today, it is available to both military personnel and civilians, with services ranging from simple ash scatterings to full-body burials conducted from a ship.

Legal Requirements and Permits

In the United States, burials at sea are regulated by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the United States Coast Guard. The EPA requires that full-body burials take place at least three nautical miles from land and in water at least 600 feet deep. Cremated remains may also be scattered at sea, but must follow similar distance requirements. Permits and notifications are typically required, and it is important to work with a licensed provider to ensure all legal aspects are met.

Types of Burial At Sea Services

Service Type Description Who Can Participate
Full-Body Burial The entire body is committed to the sea, often in a biodegradable casket or shroud. Military and civilians (with proper permits)
Ash Scattering Cremated remains are scattered on the water's surface. Anyone
Unattended Service The provider performs the ceremony without family present and sends a certificate of location. Families unable to attend in person

Planning the Ceremony

Planning a burial at sea involves several steps. First, select a reputable provider experienced in maritime funerals. Decide whether you prefer a full-body burial or ash scattering. Coordinate with the provider to arrange transportation, necessary permits, and the type of vessel. Many families choose to include readings, prayers, or music as part of the ceremony. Some services offer video recordings or live streams for those unable to attend in person.

Environmental Considerations

Burial at sea is often chosen for its environmental benefits. Biodegradable caskets and urns are used to minimize impact on marine life. The EPA regulates materials to ensure they break down naturally in the ocean. Families are encouraged to avoid non-biodegradable items such as plastic flowers or wreaths.
