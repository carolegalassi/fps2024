What Is Human Cremation?

Cremation is a method of final disposition that uses high heat and flame to reduce the human body to bone fragments, which are then processed into a fine, sand-like consistency called cremated remains or ashes. This process is performed in a specially designed furnace known as a cremation chamber. Cremation has become an increasingly popular choice due to its simplicity, flexibility, and often lower cost compared to traditional burial.

Step-by-Step Cremation Process

  1. Preparation: The body is identified, and all necessary paperwork and authorizations are completed. Medical devices or jewelry are removed as required.
  2. Placement: The body is placed in a cremation container, which is typically made of wood or cardboard and is combustible.
  3. Cremation: The container is placed in the cremation chamber, where it is exposed to intense heat (usually between 1400 and 1800 degrees Fahrenheit) for two to three hours.
  4. Processing: After cooling, the remaining bone fragments are processed into fine ashes.
  5. Return of Remains: The ashes are placed in a temporary container or an urn and returned to the family.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is cremation accepted by all religions?

Many religions permit cremation, but some, such as Orthodox Judaism and Islam, generally prohibit it. It is important to consult with your faith leader if you have concerns about religious guidelines.

What can be done with the ashes?

Ashes can be kept in an urn, scattered in a meaningful location, placed in a columbarium, or even incorporated into keepsakes or jewelry. Some families choose to divide the ashes among relatives.

Can there still be a funeral service?

Absolutely. Cremation does not prevent you from holding a memorial or funeral service. Services can be held before or after the cremation, with or without the body present.
