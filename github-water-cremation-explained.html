Water Cremation Explained

Water cremation, also known as alkaline hydrolysis or resomation, is gaining attention as a modern, environmentally friendly alternative to traditional cremation and burial. This page provides a comprehensive overview of water cremation, including how it works, its benefits, and how it compares to other end-of-life options. Whether you are considering this process for yourself or a loved one, understanding the facts can help you make an informed decision.

What Is Water Cremation?

Water cremation, scientifically referred to as alkaline hydrolysis, is a process that uses a combination of water, heat, and alkaline chemicals to accelerate the natural decomposition of the body. Unlike flame-based cremation, this method does not rely on fire, making it a gentler and more eco-conscious option. The process results in a sterile liquid and bone remains, which are then processed into a fine powder and returned to the family, similar to traditional cremated ashes.

How Does Water Cremation Work?

The body is placed in a specialized stainless steel chamber. The chamber is filled with a solution of water and potassium hydroxide, then heated to a high temperature under pressure. Over several hours, the solution breaks down the body tissues, leaving only the bone fragments. These fragments are then dried and processed into a powder, which is returned to the family in an urn. The remaining liquid is sterile and can be safely returned to the environment.

Benefits of Water Cremation

One of the main advantages of water cremation is its reduced environmental impact. The process uses significantly less energy than flame cremation and does not release harmful greenhouse gases or mercury into the atmosphere. Families who are concerned about their ecological footprint often choose this method for its sustainability. Additionally, water cremation is a gentle process that many find to be a more respectful alternative to traditional methods.

Availability and Considerations

While water cremation is legal in an increasing number of states and countries, availability may vary depending on local regulations. It is important to check with local funeral providers to determine if this option is offered in your area. Costs for water cremation are often comparable to or slightly higher than flame cremation, but many families feel the environmental and ethical benefits are worth the investment.
