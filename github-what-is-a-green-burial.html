What Is A Green Burial?

Green burial, also known as natural burial, is an environmentally conscious alternative to traditional burial practices. It is designed to minimize environmental impact by forgoing chemical embalming, metal caskets, and concrete vaults in favor of biodegradable materials and natural processes. As more families seek sustainable and meaningful ways to honor their loved ones, green burial is becoming an increasingly popular choice across North America and beyond.

Understanding Green Burial

At its core, a green burial is about returning the body to the earth as naturally as possible. This approach avoids the use of toxic chemicals, non-biodegradable materials, and energy-intensive processes. Instead, the deceased is typically placed in a simple shroud or a biodegradable casket made from materials like untreated wood, wicker, or even cardboard. The grave is dug by hand or with minimal machinery, and the burial site is often left in a natural state, sometimes marked only by a native plant or a simple stone.

Key Features of Green Burial

  • No embalming with toxic chemicals
  • Use of biodegradable caskets or shrouds
  • No concrete burial vaults or liners
  • Minimal disturbance to the natural landscape
  • Graves often marked with natural objects or GPS coordinates

Green Burial vs. Traditional Burial

Environmental Benefits

Green burial offers significant environmental advantages. By skipping embalming and using biodegradable materials, the process reduces soil and water contamination. The absence of concrete vaults and metal caskets means less resource extraction and manufacturing pollution. Additionally, green cemeteries often serve as protected natural areas, supporting local ecosystems and wildlife. Families can take comfort in knowing their loved one’s final resting place contributes to land conservation and ecological restoration.

How to Arrange a Green Burial

Arranging a green burial involves several key steps. First, locate a cemetery or burial ground that supports green burials. Many cemeteries now offer dedicated natural burial sections. Next, choose a biodegradable casket or shroud. Work with a funeral provider familiar with eco-friendly practices, and discuss your wishes with family members.
