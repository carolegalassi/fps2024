What Is Water Cremation?

Water cremation, also known as aquamation or alkaline hydrolysis, is a modern alternative to traditional flame-based cremation. This innovative process uses water, heat, and alkaline chemicals to return the body to its basic elements in a gentle and environmentally friendly way. As more families seek sustainable end-of-life options, water cremation is gaining attention for its minimal environmental impact and respectful approach.

How Does Water Cremation Work?

Unlike traditional cremation, which uses intense flame to break down the body, water cremation relies on a combination of water and alkaline chemicals. The process takes place in a specialized stainless steel vessel. The body is placed inside, and a solution of 95% water and 5% alkali is added. The vessel is then heated to around 300 degrees Fahrenheit, but at much lower temperatures than flame cremation. Over several hours, the solution gently dissolves organic material, leaving behind only bone fragments and a sterile liquid.

Key Steps in the Aquamation Process

  • The body is respectfully placed in the aquamation chamber.
  • A water and alkali solution is added and heated.
  • Over 6-8 hours, the process breaks down tissues, leaving only bone and liquid.
  • The liquid is safely returned to the environment, and the bone fragments are processed into a fine powder, similar to cremated ashes.

Comparing Water Cremation and Flame Cremation

Aspect Water Cremation Traditional Cremation
Process Uses water, alkali, and heat Uses high heat and flame
Environmental Impact Low emissions, energy efficient Higher emissions, more energy
Remains Powdered bone, similar to ashes Cremated ashes

Benefits of Water Cremation

Families are choosing water cremation for several reasons. The process is gentle and uses fewer resources than flame cremation. It produces no direct greenhouse gas emissions and avoids the release of mercury or other pollutants. The sterile liquid byproduct is safe for municipal water systems, and the resulting remains can be returned to loved ones in an urn, just like traditional ashes.

Water cremation is also accepted by many faiths and cultures, though it is important to check local regulations and religious guidelines. As awareness grows, more funeral homes are offering this service as part of their eco-friendly options.
