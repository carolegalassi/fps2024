What Happens at a Jewish Funeral: Traditions and Process

Jewish funerals are deeply rooted in tradition and guided by centuries-old customs that honor the deceased and comfort the bereaved. If you are attending a Jewish funeral for the first time, or simply want to understand what happens at a Jewish funeral, this guide will walk you through the key rituals, etiquette, and the sequence of events you can expect. From preparation to burial, each step is designed to show respect and provide a meaningful farewell.

Overview of Jewish Funeral Customs

Jewish funeral practices are guided by the principles of simplicity, dignity, and respect for the deceased. The process typically begins soon after death, with the goal of burial taking place as quickly as possible, often within 24 hours. This tradition is rooted in Jewish law and is intended to honor the body and soul of the departed.

Preparation includes a ritual washing of the body, known as taharah, performed by a group called the chevra kadisha. The deceased is dressed in simple white shrouds, called tachrichim, and placed in a plain wooden casket. Embalming and open casket viewings are not part of traditional Jewish funerals.

Sequence of Events at a Jewish Funeral

The funeral service is typically held at a synagogue, funeral home, or graveside. It is led by a rabbi or a knowledgeable community member. The service often includes readings from Psalms, prayers, a eulogy, and the recitation of the Kaddish prayer. Family members may participate by sharing memories or reading passages.

After the service, the funeral procession moves to the cemetery for burial. Mourners may participate in placing earth into the grave, a powerful act symbolizing both finality and respect. The immediate family begins the period of mourning, known as shiva, following the burial.

Etiquette and What to Expect

At a Jewish funeral, it is customary to dress modestly and wear subdued colors. Men often wear a head covering, such as a kippah, which is usually provided at the entrance. Visitors are encouraged to offer condolences but should avoid making small talk with the immediate family during the service. Silence and reflection are valued, and participation in prayers is welcomed but not required for non-Jewish guests.

After the burial, mourners may gather at the family home for shiva, a week-long period of mourning. Visitors may bring food or make donations to charity in memory of the deceased. Sharing memories and offering support are important aspects of this time.
