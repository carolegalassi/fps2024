What Happens to Body in Autopsy: Step-by-Step Guide

An autopsy is a thorough examination of a body after death, performed by a forensic pathologist to determine the cause and manner of death. Many families wonder what exactly happens to the body in autopsy, and understanding the process can provide clarity and peace of mind. This guide explains the steps, procedures, and respectful care involved during an autopsy.

Understanding the Autopsy Process

The autopsy process is performed with dignity and respect for the deceased. It typically begins after legal permissions are obtained and the body is transported to a medical examiner's facility. The main goals are to identify the cause of death, document injuries or diseases, and collect evidence if necessary. Autopsies are often requested in cases of unexpected, unexplained, or suspicious deaths, but may also be performed for medical research or educational purposes.

Key Steps in an Autopsy

  • External Examination: The pathologist inspects the body for external signs of trauma, disease, or identifying marks. Photographs and detailed notes are taken.
  • Internal Examination: A Y-shaped incision is made to access the chest and abdominal cavities. Organs are carefully removed, weighed, and examined for abnormalities.
  • Brain Examination: The scalp is opened and the skull is carefully cut to examine the brain, which is then inspected for signs of injury or disease.
  • Sample Collection: Tissue samples, fluids, and sometimes entire organs are collected for further laboratory analysis, including toxicology and histology.
  • Documentation: All findings are meticulously recorded, and a comprehensive autopsy report is prepared.
  • Restoration: After examination, the organs are either returned to the body or preserved, and the incisions are sutured. The body is cleaned and prepared for release to the funeral home.

Respect and Dignity During Autopsy

Throughout the autopsy, the body is treated with utmost respect. Only authorized personnel are present, and procedures are performed with care to preserve the appearance of the deceased. After the examination, the body is reconstructed and cleaned, making it suitable for viewing and traditional funeral services.

Common Questions About Autopsies

  • Will the body be disfigured? No, pathologists take great care to restore the body so that it can be viewed by family and friends.
  • How long does an autopsy take? Most autopsies are completed within 2 to 4 hours, though lab results may take longer.
  • Are families informed of the findings? Yes, families can request a copy of the autopsy report from the medical examiner's office.
